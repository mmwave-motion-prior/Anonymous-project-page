Abstract

Millimeter-wave (mmWave) radar offers a privacy-preserving solution for human motion reconstruction in visually challenging environments, yet practical deployment is still constrained by the scarcity of paired radar-motion data. Existing real-world mmWave datasets are typically small, environment-specific, and expensive to collect, which limits generalization to new rooms, subjects, and sensing conditions. Although simulation provides scalable supervision, transferring simulated radar measurements to real deployments remains difficult due to multipath, clutter, hardware noise, and distance-dependent resolution degradation.

We propose mmMoPrior, a sim-to-real framework that learns transferable simulation priors for data-efficient real-world mmWave human motion reconstruction. mmMoPrior builds a large-scale corpus of simulated multi-modal mmWave heatmaps paired with AMASS-driven human motion and pretrains radar-measurement and motion representations from synthetic supervision. To reduce the residual measurement gap, we introduce physics-informed domain randomization in heatmap space and perform lightweight target-environment calibration with only a small amount of real data. Within this framework, we investigate both continuous regression and tokenized motion prediction, revealing a consistent robustness-precision trade-off: tokenized prediction provides stronger zero-shot robustness under severe deployment shift, whereas continuous regression benefits more from calibration and delivers the best final reconstruction accuracy.

Experiments across multiple real-world environments, distances, subjects, poses, and motion categories show that simulation priors can substantially reduce real-data requirements for practical mmWave human motion reconstruction, enabling effective adaptation to unseen environments with as few as 24 real sequences (about one minute of calibration data).
